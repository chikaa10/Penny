01 Overview

Banking apps show you data.
Penny tells you what it means.

I open my banking app maybe once a week. I see my balance, feel vaguely fine or vaguely stressed, and close it. That's the experience for most people, and it's not because they don't care about money. It's because the app gives them no signal, just noise. Penny is my attempt to answer: what if it actually helped?

The Problem

Banking apps are archives, not advisors. They tell you what happened. Never what it means or what to do next.

The Opportunity

Build an AI layer that feels like a smart friend who knows your finances. Not a chatbot, not a dashboard. Just a quiet nudge when it counts.

02 Design Process

I used AI to design the process,
not just the product.

Before a single screen existed, I spent time understanding what people actually feel about their money. I used AI throughout, not to automate decisions, but to move faster through the messy parts so I could spend more time on the parts that actually matter.

1
Competitive Audit
Went deep on 6 banking apps: TD, RBC, Scotiabank, Monzo, Revolut, Mint. Looked at 28 things across onboarding, navigation, how they show data, and whether any of them actually use AI in a meaningful way. Spoiler: most don't.
2
Research & Synthesis ✦ AI-assisted
Talked to 6 people aged 25–42 with different relationships to money. Used Claude to help cluster the transcripts. What took 8 hours of affinity mapping compressed into about 2. The themes that came out weren't surprising, but hearing people say them out loud was.
3
Jobs-to-Be-Done Mapping
Turned the research into 4 real jobs people are hiring a banking app to do. For each one, I mapped where the current experience breaks down, and figured out where Penny's AI could step in without feeling like an interruption.
4
IA & Content Strategy ✦ AI-assisted
Designing the information architecture for something with 40+ data types is brutal. I used AI to generate copy alternatives for the sensitive stuff: overdraft language, AI nudges. and tested the tone on real users. Turns out wording matters enormously when it's someone's money.
5
Interaction Design & Motion
Wrote full motion specs for all 3 flows: spring curves, timing, how errors and empty states behave. Designed to feel native iOS, not like a web app that got ported over. The details here are what separate something that feels good from something that just works.
6
Prototype & Usability Testing
Built a click-through prototype and ran it past 5 people. Three things broke. I fixed them. The testing wasn't about proving the concept worked; it was about finding out where it didn't.

04 Penny Intelligence

The hardest part wasn't the AI.
It was earning the right to use it.

I spent more time designing trust than I did designing features. Before Penny could tell someone their dining spend is too high, it had to prove it was worth listening to. Every principle below came from that question: how do we earn it?

Transparency
Penny always shows its receipts: which transactions triggered the insight
People said they'd distrust advice they couldn't verify. So I made the reasoning visible every time.
Optionality
One tap to dismiss anything. Always.
Penny has opinions, but it never traps you. The moment it feels like a nag, people stop listening.
Plain English
No financial jargon. Ever. If a 12-year-old couldn't understand it, we rewrote it.
Tested copy across 3 different financial literacy levels. Simpler always won.
Timing
Penny speaks after transactions or weekly. Never mid-task.
Interrupting someone mid-payment to comment on their spending? That's how you get uninstalled.
Calibration
You set Penny's sensitivity during setup, not buried in settings
People trust AI more when they feel like they chose it. This was one of the clearest insights from testing.
Ambient Position
Penny lives below the fold. Present, but not in charge.
It's your money, your app. Penny is a guest, not the host.
"
The AI can't be smarter than the user's trust in it. I kept coming back to that. Before Penny could give advice, it had to earn the right, one small, honest interaction at a time.

05 Prototype Flows

Three flows.
Each one a different kind of trust moment.

I designed in reverse, starting with the moments that mattered most to users, then working backwards to how they'd get there. Every flow is about earning a little more trust than the last.

01
Onboarding
The setup that earns permission
12 screens · Target: 3.2 min · Drop-off target: <18% at step 4
Most onboarding treats AI like a feature you unlock. I treated it like a conversation you have. Before Penny gets any access, it explains what it's going to do, and lets you decide how much of it you want.
  • The sensitivity slider ('Just show me data' → 'Keep me ahead') was the most-loved screen in testing. People felt like they were in control before they'd even started
  • First insight appears immediately after account linking. Not a promise of future value. Actual value, now.
  • Face ID and OAuth before passwords, which reduces friction at the moments people most want to give up
02
Weekly Review: Hero Flow
Your week in under 90 seconds
7 screens · Target: <90 sec · The flow I'm most proud of
Every Sunday evening, Penny gives you a card-by-card digest of your week: what went well, what to watch, one thing to do. It's designed to feel like a quick chat with a friend, not a performance review of your spending habits.
  • Card swipe with velocity tracking. If you swipe fast, it knows you're just moving through; slow down and it pauses
  • Weekly, not daily. People in research said daily money check-ins gave them anxiety; Sunday felt natural
  • The completion animation is 1.2 seconds of confetti. Subtle. Earnable. People loved it in testing.
03
Spend Anomaly
The alert that doesn't feel like an alert
7 screens · Target: <45 sec · The highest-stakes trust moment
When you've spent 1.3× more than usual on something, Penny surfaces a card. Not a push notification, not a red badge. An amber card, calm language, and three options: understand it, set a soft limit, or dismiss. That's it.
  • Amber, not red. The colour alone changed how people described the feature in testing. Red felt like an accusation. Amber felt like a heads-up.
  • 'Explain more' opens a conversational view where Penny talks you through it in plain English, not a chart
  • Once dismissed, the anomaly card disappears from home completely. No residual anxiety.
06 Design Decisions

The decisions that
weren't obvious.

What I chose
What I almost did instead
Why I went this way
Cards on the home screen, not push alerts
Real-time push notifications for spending anomalies
In research, people described bank push notifications as either ignored or alarming. Never useful. A card on the home screen waits for the user, not the other way around.
Sensitivity slider in onboarding, not settings
Full AI on by default, with an off switch buried in settings
People engage more with AI they chose than AI they tolerate. Surfacing the slider in onboarding made it a feature, not a disclaimer.
Sunday weekly digest only
Daily spending summaries
Daily money check-ins created anxiety in every person I talked to. Weekly matched how people actually think about their finances: in week-sized chunks, not day-sized ones.
Show the transactions that triggered the insight
Just show the conclusion ("you spent more on dining")
People said they'd dismiss anything they couldn't verify. Showing your work isn't just good ethics. It's good UX.
One sentence of plain text over a chart
Mini data visualizations for every AI insight
For the 30–40 age group I was designing for, a clear sentence beat a graph they had to interpret every single time in testing.

If I had another month

  • Android version, because the trust patterns I found probably look different on Material You
  • A/B test: one sentence of text vs. a mini sparkline for anomaly cards
  • Design for the stressful moments (job loss, unexpected bills), not just steady-state users
  • Run a full VoiceOver walkthrough with a blind tester. The accessibility annotations are there, but they haven't been tested with a real user yet

What I'd take to any fintech team

  • Design the trust layer before the feature layer. Every time.
  • Mobile interaction design is physics, not just pixels. Get the spring curves right.
  • Using AI in your process isn't cheating. It's just faster affinity mapping.
  • The best AI UX is the one that disappears. If people notice it, something's wrong.
